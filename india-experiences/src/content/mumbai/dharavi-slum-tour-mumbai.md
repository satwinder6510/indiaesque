Tours follow strict no-photography policies and are led by local residents who provide authentic perspectives on life in Asia's largest slum.**

Dharavi spans 535 acres between Mahim and Sion stations, home to over one million residents who've created a thriving informal economy worth $1 billion annually. The area processes 80% of Mumbai's plastic waste, produces leather goods exported globally, and houses thousands of small-scale industries.

## Responsible Tour Operators

Reality Tours & Travel remains the gold standard for ethical Dharavi tourism. Founded in 2006 by Krishna Pujari, a former Dharavi resident, the company employs only local guides and invests profits directly into community projects. Their ₹1,400 ($17) walking tours run daily at 9:15 AM, 10:15 AM, and 2:15 PM.

The company operates NGO Reality Gives, which funds schools, vocational training programs, and healthcare initiatives within Dharavi. Over 15 years, they've educated 2,000+ children and provided skills training to 500+ women.

Dharavi Dream Tours, run by local resident Ravi Sansi, offers smaller group experiences for ₹1,200 ($14). While less established than Reality Tours, they maintain similar ethical standards and no-photography policies.

Avoid tour operators offering photography permissions or promising "authentic poverty experiences" - these exploit residents for tourist entertainment.

## What You'll See

Tours cover four main zones showcasing different aspects of community life. The recycling area demonstrates how residents sort and process 4,000 tons of waste daily, turning discarded plastic into pellets sold to manufacturers across India.

The pottery district produces clay goods using traditional techniques passed down through generations. Tamil families migrated here in the 1960s, establishing workshops that now supply restaurants throughout Mumbai.

Leather production zones house tanneries processing buffalo hide into bags, belts, and shoes exported to Europe and America. The smell is intense, but the craftsmanship is remarkable.

Residential areas reveal how families maximize space in 10x10 foot rooms housing 6-8 people. Multi-story buildings reach five floors high, connected by narrow passages that form the neighborhood's social fabric.

## Understanding the Context

Dharavi isn't just poverty - it's urban innovation. Residents pay taxes, run businesses, and maintain complex social networks. The area generates annual revenue exceeding many Indian cities, with literacy rates reaching 69%.

Government redevelopment plans threaten to displace communities that have lived here for decades. The proposed Dharavi Redevelopment Project would relocate residents to high-rise apartments while commercial developers profit from the valuable land.

Many residents oppose redevelopment, preferring incremental improvements to their existing homes over relocation. They've invested life savings in businesses and properties that redevelopment would destroy.

## Tour Guidelines and Ethics

Never take photos, even if residents seem agreeable. This policy protects children who can't consent to having their images used in tourist marketing materials.

Don't give money directly to individuals, as this creates dependency and inequality within the community. Instead, support organizations that provide long-term development assistance.

Avoid bringing donations like clothes or school supplies unless coordinated through established NGOs. Random distribution disrupts local economies and creates waste management problems.

Listen to your guide rather than making assumptions about what you observe. Many structures that appear temporary have housed families for 30+ years and represent significant investments.

## Getting There

Take the Central Line to Mahim Junction station. Reality Tours meets participants at the Times Square building on the west side. The area is well-connected by [Mumbai's transport network](/mumbai/transport/), with buses and auto-rickshaws providing additional access points.

Tours start promptly, so arrive 15 minutes early. The company provides bottled water and takes small groups of 8-12 people maximum to minimize disruption to daily life.
